The paper **"Minimal Recognizable Configurations Elicit Category-selective Responses in Higher Order Visual Cortex"** by Shimon Ullman et al. introduces a compelling concept: the Minimally Recognizable Configuration (MIRC). A MIRC is the smallest visual configuration of image components that still remains reliably recognizable as belonging to a specific category.

**[Project page at the Weizmann Institute](https://www.weizmann.ac.il/math/dannyh/minimal-recognizable-configurations-mircs-project)**

What I found particularly interesting is the question it poses: how much can we reduce, crop, or degrade an image before it becomes unrecognizable? The paper investigates this boundary systematically, probing the limits of visual generalization in both humans and computational models.

The findings connect directly to how the brain achieves robust object recognition despite large changes in viewpoint, lighting, and image quality. From a computational perspective, MIRCs also serve as a useful benchmark for testing whether models truly capture the essential features of a category, or whether they are picking up on statistical patterns in training data that don't generalise well.
